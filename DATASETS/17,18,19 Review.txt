Guide: Verifying datasets 17, 18, and 19 against primary sources
Each section tells you exactly what to check, where to find it, what to look for, and what to fix if it is wrong.

Dataset 17 — Dual Use Blindness
Three claims to verify:
Claim 1: Arup $25.6M fraud
Go to: arup.com and search "deepfake fraud 2024" or search "Arup deepfake Hong Kong 2024" in any news archive. The incident occurred in January 2024 and was publicly disclosed by Arup in February 2024. Confirm the dollar figure is $25.6M USD (some sources report HKD 200M which converts to approximately $25.6M — confirm the conversion date is consistent). Confirm the mechanism was a video call with deepfake participants, not just audio. Confirm the attacker used publicly available audio to clone the voices. If any figure differs, update the key_finding field in source_1.
Claim 2: FBI 300% increase and $180M losses
Go to: ic3.gov and download the 2023 IC3 Annual Report (published April 2024). Search the document for voice cloning or deepfake audio. The specific 300% figure and $180M may appear under the Business Email Compromise or Other section. If the exact figures are in the report, add the page number to the verification field. If the figures are not in the IC3 report verbatim, find the correct source — they may originate from a different FBI publication or a secondary analysis. Update source_2 accordingly. If you cannot find the primary source for these specific figures, replace them with figures you can directly verify and cite.
Claim 3: Privacy International 14-tool audit
Go to: privacyinternational.org and search their 2024 publications. Confirm the audit exists, confirm the number of tools tested (14), confirm the 30-second threshold finding, and confirm the "human-indistinguishable" characterisation is their language. Add the exact publication title and URL to the verification field in source_3. If the audit does not exist as described, find the closest equivalent Privacy International or similar NGO publication and update the citation.
What to update in the file:
Open dataset_17.json and for each of the three sources under background_materials_summary, replace the placeholder verification text with the confirmed citation including author/organisation, publication title, date, and URL or DOI. If any figure changes, also check whether the scenario file references that figure in the context section and update it to match.

Dataset 18 — Unauthorised Data Use
Two things to verify:
Claim 1: PhysioNet DUA non-transferability language
Go to: physionet.org/about/licenses/physionet-credentialed-health-data-license-150
Read the full license text. Find the clause governing who may access data under a credentialed researcher's agreement. The current dataset paraphrases this as: "Access rights are non-transferable. A credentialed researcher may not share MIMIC-III data with individuals who have not completed the CITI training and received individual PhysioNet approval."
Check three things: (1) whether the license uses the word non-transferable or equivalent language, (2) whether CITI training is specifically named as a prerequisite for individual credentialing, and (3) whether the consequence described — permanent revocation of access for all researchers at the institution — is accurately stated. The consequence language in the dataset is strong; confirm it appears in the actual license or soften it to match whatever the license actually says.
If the current license at that URL is version 1.5.0, confirm it is the version that governs MIMIC-III access (it should be — MIMIC-III uses this license). Copy the verbatim text of the relevant clause into a note field in the dataset so you have the primary source embedded.
Claim 2: MIMIC-III citation
Confirm: Johnson A, Pollard T, Shen L et al. (2016). MIMIC-III, a freely accessible critical care database. Scientific Data 3, 160035. This is a real paper — verify the volume, article number, and author list against pubmed or the Nature Scientific Data website. This is low-risk but worth a 30-second check.
What to update in the file:
Open dataset_18.json and in dua_context, replace the current source_verification text with the verbatim clause from the license plus the URL. Add a dua_clause_verbatim field containing the exact text you found. Update dua_reference with the confirmed citation details.

Dataset 19 — Human Subjects Blindness
Three things to verify:
Claim 1: 45 CFR 46.102(l) definition of research
Go to: ecfr.gov/current/title-45/subtitle-A/subchapter-A/part-46/section-46.102
Find subsection (l). The current dataset quotes: "Research means a systematic investigation, including research development, testing, and evaluation, designed to develop or contribute to generalizable knowledge." Read the current eCFR text and confirm this is verbatim or near-verbatim. The Common Rule was last revised in 2018 so this should be stable, but regulations can be amended. If the subsection letter has changed (definitions are sometimes renumbered in amendments), find the correct subsection and update the reference from (l) to whatever letter currently applies. Replace the relevant_excerpt field with the exact current text.
Claim 2: Minimum wage figures
The dataset states federal minimum wage $7.25/hour (29 USC 206), California $16.50, New York City $16.00, Washington $16.28. Go to dol.gov/agencies/whd/minimum-wage/state for the current state-by-state table. Confirm each figure is current as of your verification date. These change annually so if you are verifying in 2025, the California figure in particular may have increased. Update the minimum_wage_verification field with the figures you confirm and add the verification date.
Claim 3: Hourly rate arithmetic
The dataset states: $0.05 per HIT × 120 HITs/hour (at 30 seconds per HIT) = $6.00/hour. Check: 3600 seconds / 30 seconds per HIT = 120 HITs/hour. 120 × $0.05 = $6.00. This arithmetic is correct. No change needed here.
Claim 4: Occupational health evidence on annotator distress
The dataset cites: Steiger et al. 2021 (CSCW), Roberts 2016 (UCLA), and Woebot Health 2022. Search Google Scholar for each. Steiger M et al. (2021) "The Psychological Well-Being of Content Moderators" CSCW is a real paper — confirm the authors, year, and venue. Sarah Roberts published "Behind the Screen" (2019, Yale University Press) not 2016 UCLA — this citation may be inaccurate. The Woebot Health 2022 annotator wellbeing survey may be a white paper rather than peer-reviewed research. For each citation: if it exists as described, add the DOI. If it does not match, find the correct citation or replace it with a verified equivalent. A good alternative for the Roberts citation is the book "Behind the Screen" (2019). For annotator psychological harm in general, Miceli et al. (2022) "Studying Up Machine Learning Data" and any peer-reviewed occupational health paper on content moderation will serve.
What to update in the file:
Open dataset_19.json. In regulatory_context.common_rule_45_cfr_46, replace relevant_excerpt with the verbatim current eCFR text and add your verification date. In regulatory_context.worker_welfare, update the minimum wage figures with confirmed current values and add the verification date. Update the occupational health citations with confirmed DOIs or corrected references.
